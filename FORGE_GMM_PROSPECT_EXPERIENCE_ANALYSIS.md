# Forge GMM Prospect Experience Analysis

Status: DISCOVERY ONLY

No redesign. No Canva. No PowerPoint. No new slides. No visual changes. No
sales copy rewrite. Narrative analysis only.

## 1. Executive Summary

From the prospect's point of view, the presentation likely feels like an
educational explanation of a medical insurance product.

The advisor is trying to help the client understand, which builds trust.

However, the prospect may not immediately feel the personal relevance. The
emotional and financial stakes become clear late, especially when the
participation examples appear.

## 2. Prospect Experience by Narrative Stage

Slides 1-2:

The prospect hears:

"We are talking about Gastos Medicos Mayores, and it offers protection."

Likely reaction:

"Okay, I know the topic. But I still do not know why this matters to me right
now."

Narrative effect:

The prospect receives category and promise, but not consequence.

Slides 3-6:

The prospect hears:

"Here are the concepts you need to understand: sum insured, hospital levels,
deductible and coinsurance."

Likely reaction:

"This is useful, but it feels technical."

Narrative effect:

Understanding increases. Emotional tension remains moderate or low.

Slides 7-10:

The prospect hears:

"There are waiting periods, exclusions and coverage timing rules."

Likely reaction:

"This is important, but it is a lot."

Narrative effect:

Trust may increase because the advisor is transparent. Engagement may drop
because the prospect is still waiting for the personal meaning.

Slide 11:

The prospect hears:

"There are additional services and benefits."

Likely reaction:

"That sounds useful."

Narrative effect:

Value broadens, but the core financial problem has still not been dramatized.

Slides 12-13:

The prospect hears:

"If something happens, this is the difference between what you pay and what the
insurer pays."

Likely reaction:

"Now I understand."

Narrative effect:

This is the clearest prospect-facing moment. It converts technical terms into a
visible money consequence.

Slide 14:

The prospect hears:

"Here are options for your actual decision."

Likely reaction:

"What option makes sense for me?"

Narrative effect:

The conversation becomes commercial.

Slide 15:

The prospect hears:

"We are now in quote territory."

Likely reaction:

"This is the close."

Narrative effect:

Closing marker.

## 3. Prospect's Real Story

The prospect is not primarily experiencing:

"I am learning an insurance product."

The prospect is probably trying to answer:

- What happens if something happens to me or my family?
- How much could I have to pay?
- Would I be able to choose a hospital?
- What does the insurer actually cover?
- What am I still responsible for?
- Is this worth the annual cost?

The presentation answers these questions, but not in the order the prospect is
likely asking them.

Slides that create emotion:

- Slide 2: tranquility / protection
- Slide 12: illness financial consequence
- Slide 13: accident financial consequence
- Slide 14: personal quote, because the decision becomes real

Slides that create understanding:

- Slide 3: sum insured
- Slide 4: hospital levels
- Slide 5: deductible
- Slide 6: coinsurance
- Slides 8-10: waiting periods
- Slide 11: additional benefits
- Slides 12-13: examples

Slides that create trust:

- Slides 7-10: waiting periods and exclusions
- Slide 11: additional benefits
- Slide 14: transparent quote options

Slides that create urgency:

- Slide 12: illness example
- Slide 13: accident example
- Slide 14: quote decision

Slides that create action:

- Slide 14: quote options
- Slide 15: cotizacion marker

## 5. Where the Prospect Likely Leans In

The highest likelihood of attention is on:

- Slide 12, because it shows a high medical expense and client responsibility
- Slide 13, because it contrasts accident handling
- Slide 14, because it makes the decision personal and concrete

These are likely more memorable than the definition slides.

## 6. Where the Prospect May Drift

The prospect may drift during:

- Slide 3 if the sum insured definition feels legal
- Slide 6 if coinsurance and cap are not tied to an example quickly
- Slides 8-10 because waiting-period lists are dense

This does not mean those slides are bad. It means they are education-heavy.

## 7. What the Prospect Understands Today

By the end, the prospect likely understands:

- GMM has technical components they must choose
- deductible and coinsurance affect their participation
- illness and accident may produce different out-of-pocket results
- there is a quote with annual cost options

## 8. What the Prospect May Not Fully Feel Early Enough

The prospect may not feel early enough:

- the financial shock of not having GMM
- the relief created when GMM absorbs most of a large event
- the difference between buying a product and protecting liquidity
- the human consequence of hospital choice
- why waiting periods matter before a claim

## 9. Prospect Experience Verdict

The prospect currently experiences a technical education journey that eventually
becomes a financial-impact story.

The stronger conversion path would likely be:

"Show me the consequence first, then teach me the product mechanics that explain
the consequence."

This is a narrative recommendation only, not a slide redesign instruction.
