# Truth Resolution Candidate Contract v0.1

Status: DRAFT
Date: 2026-06-17
Version: 0.1

## 1. Purpose
This contract defines the bridge between semantic candidate evidence (generated by the Semantic Extraction Layer) and the canonical Event Ledger (the truth store). It formalizes how proposed semantic insights are evaluated, promoted, disputed, or rejected within the Forge OS Truth Resolution architecture.

## 2. Constitutional Invariant
> "Truth is adjudicated, not generated."

Semantic interpretation may expand the scope of what Forge notices, but it must never expand the scope of what Forge claims to know. The Truth Resolution engine adjudicates between competing evidence to establish canonical truth.

## 3. Accepted Evidence Sources
Evidence ingested by Truth Resolution must originate from authorized sources:
- **deterministic_extractor:** Rule-based extraction with verified provenance.
- **semantic_extractor:** Interpretive extraction generating candidate events.
- **human_input:** Direct input from the advisor.
- **external_verified_source:** Data ingested from trusted external systems (e.g., carrier feeds).

## 4. Evidence Levels
Evidence is categorized by its standing within the Truth Resolution system:
- **canonical:** Verified truth, append-only, stored in the Event Ledger.
- **candidate:** Proposed evidence awaiting adjudication.
- **disputed:** Conflicting evidence requiring resolution.
- **rejected:** Evidence deemed invalid or false; auditable but removed from canonical consideration.

## 5. Candidate Promotion Rules
A `semantic candidate` may be promoted to `canonical` status **IF AND ONLY IF** it meets one of the following criteria:
1. **Human Acceptance:** Advisor review and explicit approval via the Advisor OS.
2. **Deterministic Corroboration:** Confirmation by the primary deterministic engine.
3. **Ledger Corroboration:** Validation against existing, high-confidence ledger history.
4. **Explicit Advisor Promotion:** Direct intervention by the advisor.

## 6. Conflict Resolution
Conflicts arise when evidence sources suggest contradictory truths for the same entity or state.
*Example:* 
- Candidate evidence: `owner=advisor`
- Ledger evidence: `owner=prospect`

**Resolution:**
The system MUST flag this as `disputed evidence`. It SHALL NOT automatically resolve the conflict. It must surface both pieces of evidence (with their respective sources and confidence scores) to the human advisor for adjudication.

## 7. Audit Requirements
All promotions, rejections, and conflict resolutions must be auditable. Every change to truth state must include:
- **Actor:** Who promoted/rejected (e.g., advisor_id, system_engine_id).
- **When:** Timestamp of the action.
- **Why:** Rationale (e.g., "manual_override", "deterministic_match").
- **Source:** Original source of the candidate evidence.
- **Evidence Span:** The specific context (`evidence_span`) from which the semantic candidate was derived.

## 8. Immutability Rules
- Canonical truth is **append-only**.
- Once recorded in the Ledger, canonical events cannot be modified or deleted.
- Corrections to previous truth must be registered as *new* events, creating a historical record of the change.

## 9. Rejection Rules
- Rejected evidence is **never destroyed**.
- Rejections are recorded as events (e.g., `evidence.rejected`), ensuring the rejection itself remains auditable for future debugging and system refinement.

## 10. Relationship with Event Ledger
- **Event Ledger:** Stores the canonical truth and the audit trail of all evidence.
- **Truth Resolution Engine:** Adjudicates candidate evidence against canonical truth.
- **Semantic Extraction Layer:** Proposes candidate truth based on interpretation.

## 11. Determinism and Semantic Evidence
**Final Question: Can Truth Resolution remain deterministic while accepting semantic evidence?**

Yes. Truth Resolution remains deterministic by strictly separating the **generation** of candidate evidence (which is probabilistic) from the **adjudication** of that evidence (which is rule-based and deterministic). The semantic extractor *proposes* candidates, but the resolution engine *applies* a fixed set of deterministic rules to promote, dispute, or reject those candidates based on the established corroboration criteria. The outcome of the adjudication process for a given set of inputs is therefore reproducible and deterministic.
