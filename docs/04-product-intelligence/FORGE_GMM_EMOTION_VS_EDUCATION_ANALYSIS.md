# Forge GMM Emotion vs Education Analysis

Status: DISCOVERY ONLY

No redesign. No Canva. No PowerPoint. No new slides. No visual changes. No
sales copy rewrite. Narrative analysis only.

## 1. Executive Summary

The presentation is education-heavy before it becomes emotionally clear.

That is not inherently wrong. GMM is a technical product, and trust requires
clear explanation.

The narrative risk is that education arrives before the prospect has enough
emotional or financial context to care deeply about the definitions.

## 2. Emotion Slides

Slide 2:

Creates emotion through tranquility and protection.

Strength:
It names the emotional benefit.

Limitation:
It does not yet show the problem, the consequence or the lived experience.

Slide 12:

Creates emotion through financial contrast in illness.

Strength:
The table makes consequence visible. The prospect can see the gap between total
expense, client participation and insurer participation.

Limitation:
Because it arrives late, some prospects may have already lost emotional
attention.

Slide 13:

Creates emotion through accident comparison.

Strength:
It is highly concrete. It shows that accident coverage logic can change the
client's responsibility dramatically.

Limitation:
Like slide 12, it arrives after many technical definitions.

Slide 14:

Creates emotion through personal quote and affordability decision.

Strength:
The presentation becomes personal.

Limitation:
Price may appear before the prospect has fully internalized value if slides
12-13 were not discussed deeply.

## 3. Education Slides

Slide 3:

Teaches sum insured.

Narrative value:
Helps the prospect understand the insurer's maximum responsibility.

Risk:
May feel legal or abstract.

Slide 4:

Teaches hospital levels.

Narrative value:
Helps the prospect understand hospital access choices.

Risk:
Hospital level may matter more if linked to a human desire: where would I want
to be treated if something serious happened?

Slide 5:

Teaches deductible.

Narrative value:
Very important for understanding client participation.

Risk:
The concept becomes powerful only once paired with a claim example.

Slide 6:

Teaches coinsurance and cap.

Narrative value:
Critical to out-of-pocket understanding.

Risk:
Conceptually complex. It benefits from immediate financial demonstration.

Slides 7-10:

Teach waiting periods and exclusions.

Narrative value:
Builds credibility through transparency.

Risk:
Dense lists may interrupt emotional momentum.

Slide 11:

Teaches additional benefits.

Narrative value:
Adds service value beyond catastrophic coverage.

Risk:
May feel like feature listing if not connected to client life.

## 4. Outcome-Selling Slides

The slides that sell outcomes most clearly are:

- Slide 2: peace of mind / protection
- Slide 12: reduced financial exposure in illness
- Slide 13: reduced financial exposure in accident
- Slide 14: concrete quote decision

The most outcome-rich slides are 12 and 13.

They show:

- what happens if medical expenses appear
- what the client covers
- what the insurer covers
- why the annual premium can make sense

## 7. What Happens If I Do Not Have GMM?

The presentation implies this most clearly in slides 12 and 13.

Those slides show large medical expenses and imply that without GMM the client
could face the full cost or a much larger financial burden.

However, the presentation does not foreground this question early.

## 8. What Changes If I Do Have GMM?

Slides 12 and 13 answer this most clearly:

- the insurer absorbs a large portion of covered expenses
- the client responsibility becomes bounded by deductible, coinsurance and
  applicable rules
- accidents may behave differently from illnesses

Slide 14 adds:

- the prospect can choose a level, deductible and annual payment option

## 9. Emotion vs Education Balance

Current balance:

- Education: dominant
- Emotion: present but delayed
- Financial consequence: strong but late
- Urgency: mostly late
- Action: appears at quote stage

## 10. Discovery Conclusion

The narrative does not need less education.

It needs education to be anchored earlier in consequence.

The prospect should not first learn concepts in isolation. The prospect should
first understand why those concepts matter.
