## 1. Executive Summary

Manager friction is the force that prevents a manager from taking development
action even when they know coaching, accountability or intervention is needed.

Like prospect and advisor friction, manager friction is often a competing
motivation, not absence of motivation.

## 2. Manager Friction Types

### Difficult Conversation Avoidance

Description:

Manager delays a necessary coaching conversation because it may create conflict.

Likely hidden friction:

Desire to preserve harmony competes with responsibility to develop.

### Delayed Coaching

Description:

Manager waits until the issue becomes larger instead of intervening early.

Likely hidden friction:

Uncertainty, time pressure, avoidance or lack of confidence in coaching method.

### Unclear Expectations

Description:

Manager expects performance but has not defined the behavior, cadence or
standard clearly.

Likely hidden friction:

Assumption that the advisor "should know" what to do.

### Over-Rescuing

Description:

Manager solves for the advisor instead of developing the advisor's capability.

Likely hidden friction:

Desire for short-term result competes with long-term development.

### Evidence Avoidance

Description:

Manager relies on feeling, favoritism or frustration instead of reviewing
behavior evidence.

Likely hidden friction:

Evidence may complicate the story the manager already believes.

### Accountability Softening

Description:

Manager gives encouragement but avoids clear accountability.

Likely hidden friction:

Fear of being disliked or fear of causing disengagement.

## 3. Does Leadership Friction Follow Similar Patterns?

Yes.

Leadership friction often mirrors individual friction:

- fear of conflict
- ambiguity
- avoidance
- weak next step
- emotional discomfort
- competing incentives
- time pressure
- lack of confidence

The difference is consequence.

Manager friction affects not only the manager, but the advisor's development and
the team's operating rhythm.

## 4. Relationship to Readiness

A manager may be ready to lead in principle but blocked by friction in a
specific responsibility.

Examples:

- ready to review numbers, not ready to confront behavior
- ready to motivate, not ready to set boundaries
- ready to support, not ready to hold accountable
- ready to recruit, not ready to develop

This supports the broader readiness principle:

Readiness is responsibility-specific.

## 5. Discovery Conclusion

Manager Friction Intelligence should understand where leadership action stalls.

It should never become surveillance or punishment.

It should support better coaching, clearer expectations and earlier development
intervention.
